## Supervisor synthesis

This deck is an earlier and broader working document. It does not replace the current artefact framework, but it clarifies why the research moved from a business-model framing toward a sharper question: how AI reconfigures perceived value co-creation in management consulting by changing how managerial knowledge is produced, perceived, attributed, appropriated and mobilized.

The deck contributes six important elements to the current project:

1. It anchors the research problem in consulting as knowledge work. Management consulting is presented as a paradigmatic knowledge-work sector whose value depends on the production of explicit and tacit managerial knowledge.
2. It explains why AI is theoretically disruptive for consulting: AI directly enters the domain of knowledge production and creates ambiguity around human and artificial contributions.
3. It justifies perceived value as the right analytical lens: clients can value only what they perceive, while human-AI systems blur who contributed what.
4. It connects the model to service-dominant logic and the spheres of value creation: AI can remain in separate consultant/client spheres or become a visible resource in the joint sphere of interaction.
5. It introduces the early S1/S2/S3 intuition: S1 as human dyad, S2 as siloed/invisible AI use, S3 as visible tripartite consultant-client-AI interaction.
6. It strengthens the link with managerial knowledge: the value of consulting is tied not only to deliverables, but to the client's ability to appropriate knowledge for managerial problem solving.

## Core research trajectory in the deck

### Initial title and reframing

The opening slide shows a move away from the initial title:

> La valeur percue du conseil : impact de l'adoption de l'IA generative sur le modele d'affaires associe.

Toward a more precise research orientation:

> Vers une reconfiguration de la valeur percue du conseil en management a l'ere de l'IA ? Repenser la co-creation de connaissances manageriales.

The comments also mention a possible title around "la construction de la valeur percue du conseil en management dans les interactions consultant-client-IA : une approche par la transparence mediee".

This is directly relevant to the current artefact because it confirms that the core object is not the consulting business model itself, but the perceived-value mechanism through consultant-client-AI interactions.

### Why perceived value in consulting?

The deck frames consulting as one of the main incarnations of knowledge work, drawing on Alvesson (1995), and states that AI challenges the sector because exploitation and production of knowledge are precisely AI's domain.

It also notes that consulting's main value-creation potential lies in generating managerial knowledge, including tacit managerial knowledge (Ciampi, 2017), but that this potential is often underexploited. The deck argues that the transition from an expert consultant model to a co-creation model of managerial knowledge is rarely conscious and therefore poorly operationalized.

This point supports the current artefact's practitioner relevance: AI-augmented consulting cannot be assessed only through productivity. The question is whether augmented production becomes perceived, appropriated and useful value for the client.

### AI and blurred attribution

The deck emphasizes that human-AI systems blur the contributions of actors. It uses Raisch and Krakowski (2020) to formulate the problem: no single actor has full control over outputs, making responsibility and contribution difficult to assign.

It then links this to perceived value through Bowman and Ambrosini: clients cannot value what they do not perceive. This is an important foundation for the current emphasis on visibility, attribution and appropriation.

The deck also mobilizes Harfouche et al. (2023): human-informed AI can increase organizational knowledge. The theoretical tension is therefore not simply negative. AI can augment knowledge production, but may also make contributions harder to perceive and attribute.

## Theoretical foundations present in the deck

### Marketing and service-dominant logic

The deck combines marketing and information-systems perspectives.

From service-dominant logic and Gronroos & Voima (2013), it retains:

- value is created by the customer through the relationship and partly through direct interactions with the service provider;
- the joint sphere provides a platform for value co-creation;
- interactions between actors, rather than isolated actors, should be the unit of analysis.

This is important for the current S1/S2/S3 framework. The deck explains that:

- in S2, AI is used in separated provider/customer spheres as an invisible operant resource;
- in S3, AI becomes present in the joint sphere as a shared artefact interpreted and evaluated in interaction;
- this transition changes AI's status from internal resource to interactional resource.

### Perceived value

The deck uses Riviere & Mencarelli (2012) to describe perceived value as comparative, personal, contextual and dynamic. It also uses Gronroos & Voima (2013) to emphasize that value can be positive or negative depending on whether the customer feels in a better or worse situation through service-related experiences.

For the consulting context, the deck cites Bourgoin (2014): the value of a consulting service must be produced, communicated, defined, measured, exchanged and transformed in concrete operations. These operations amount to a "mise en valeur" of the consulting service, not as superficial communication but as an activity carrying additional justification.

This supports the current choice to translate "valuation" as "mise en valeur" in French.

### Consulting models

The deck contrasts three consulting models:

- expert model: diagnosis and treatment, asymmetrical knowledge relation, consultant authority;
- reflective practitioner model: knowledge is socially produced in consultant-client interaction; transfer requires open, minimally defensive interaction and reciprocal exploration;
- critical model: consultants as impression managers who may make clients dependent on managerial fashions.

The deck notes that no model is fully satisfactory and that a synthesis model able to account for AI remains to be defined. This supports the current framework's attempt to synthesize relational, processual and cognitive dimensions.

### Managerial knowledge

The deck defines managerial knowledge through the idea that knowledge is information converted to solve a problem. It also draws on Ciampi (2017) to argue that consulting can create explicit and tacit managerial knowledge, such as new interpretive capacities, diagnostic skills and perspectives for solving managerial problems.

This directly supports the current wording that value emerges when produced knowledge is appropriated and mobilized in managerial action.

## S1/S2/S3 in the deck

The deck presents an early S1/S2/S3 model:

### S1

- Interaction: exclusively human.
- Role of AI: absent or marginal.
- Knowledge loop: purely human, based on intuition and internal knowledge.
- Value: rests on the human relation and social recognition of consultant expertise.

### S2

- Interaction: human-centered.
- Role of AI: tool used in silo, invisible or silent.
- Knowledge loop: AI enriches human reflection cycles without initiating them.
- Value: efficiency may be increased, but attribution between consultant, AI and client becomes blurred.

### S3

- Interaction: reconfigured by AI.
- Role of AI: visible and proactive co-agent.
- Knowledge loop: AI directly influences the recursive loop and introduces new knowledge.
- Value: emerges from dynamic co-construction between consultant, client and AI.
- Perceived value is framed as the outcome of a novel co-creation process between consultant, AI and client.

The deck states that the relational structure S1/S2/S3 is a strong determinant of perceived value in AI-era consulting.

For the current artefact, this source should be used as a historical and conceptual foundation. The current artefact refines the wording: S1 is "No AI", S2 "AI in silo", and S3 "Tripartite co-creation"; S3 should not necessarily imply real-time AI orchestration, since asynchronous agentic AI can also participate in co-creation.

## Methodological elements

The deck describes a multi-phase research strategy:

1. Phase 0: informal scoping interviews with marketing/IS researchers, consultants and clients.
2. Phase 1: literature review and initial propositions/model.
3. Phase 2.1: exploratory fieldwork in a private research laboratory (`Erdos Research`) with domain experts using AI.
4. Phase 2.2: real-world case study in a consulting firm, not yet identified at the time of the deck.

The scoping interviews led to three observations:

- when AI is used in consulting services, it is mostly opaque, both for consultant and client;
- consultants sense AI's transformation potential but remain convinced of their own value;
- value remains a vague concept in consulting despite being central.

The deck also mentions a qualitative exploratory field with around 13 semi-structured interviews, addressing deliverables, knowledge, co-creation, perceived value and perspectives on AI.

## Experimental simulation insight

The final slide reports an experimental AI-based simulation of interview data. This must not be treated as empirical proof. It suggests two hypotheses:

- perceived value of human work may shift toward devaluation even when AI increases productivity and creativity, because human contribution or expertise becomes harder to attribute in the delivered result;
- there may also be a recursive knowledge-augmentation loop in human-AI exchange, beyond automation and productivity effects.

This is useful for the chatbot as a bounded exploratory insight, but it must be clearly labelled as a simulation, not as validated empirical evidence.

## Additional analytical material: problem clarity and client maturity

The annex proposes a two-axis matrix:

- problem clarity: clear vs fuzzy managerial problem;
- client maturity: low vs high maturity.

Four consulting situations are suggested:

- Investigation: fuzzy problem and low maturity; the consultant structures inquiry to clarify the situation.
- Exploration: fuzzy problem and higher maturity; the client seeks to understand a still ambiguous situation through dialogue with the consultant.
- Activation: clear problem and high maturity; the client understands the issue and seeks leverage through consulting.
- Delegation: clear problem and low maturity; the client lacks means or time and expects a more turnkey solution.

This matrix is not part of the current artefact's core model, but it may help practical extrapolations. It can be used cautiously in generative chatbot mode, not as a validated proposition of the current framework.

## Implications and limitations identified in the deck

### Epistemological implications

The deck proposes integrating recursive theory (Harfouche et al., 2023) with value co-creation spheres from service-dominant logic (Gronroos & Voima, 2013), in order to contribute to the realization of consulting's knowledge-creation potential (Ciampi, 2017) in the AI era.

It also aims to provide a new analytical framework for perceived value in consulting based on consultant-client-AI interactions as a factor of managerial knowledge creation.

### Practitioner implications

The deck frames the research as a way to help consulting firm managers understand the deeper implications of AI for consulting work, beyond the narrow model of an augmented consultant serving an augmented client.

It also connects the research to possible new consulting business models adapted to changing client expectations in an AI adoption context.

### Limitations

The deck explicitly notes limited generalizability due to the case-study method.

## Coherence with the current artefact

The deck is coherent with the current artefact on the following points:

- perceived value is not automatic and cannot be reduced to production efficiency;
- consulting value is tied to managerial knowledge creation and appropriation;
- AI blurs contribution and attribution in human-AI systems;
- S1/S2/S3 is an observational framework for different consultant-client-AI configurations;
- the model should account for both value amplification and value erosion.

The deck is less refined than the current artefact on the following points:

- it still uses broader business-model language that the current artefact treats as an implication rather than the core object;
- it sometimes frames S3 as AI "co-agent pro-actif", whereas the current artefact should avoid overcommitting to real-time or autonomous AI as a defining criterion;
- it presents early propositions that have since been refined into P1, P2 and P3;
- it includes simulated data, which should be used only as exploratory material.
